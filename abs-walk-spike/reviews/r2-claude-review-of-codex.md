# Round 2 — Claude's Review of Codex (ABS Walk Spike)

## Headline assessment

Codex's Round 2 work is technically clean and the implementation is, in most respects, well-engineered. Their LightGBM zone classifier calibrates near-perfectly (max deciles deviation 0.0028 per `findings.json:10`), the 10-seed refit ensemble shows tiny cross-seed spread (34.5% – 35.7%, see `charts/model_diagnostics/h3_ensemble_variance.png`), and their H1 estimate (+0.66pp [+0.27, +1.04]) matches mine within rounding (mine: +0.68pp [+0.31, +1.04]). I do **not** trust Codex's headline of `+35.3%` zone attribution as the right number to publish, but I now believe their framing is closer to the publication-correct one than my −64.6%. The two results are not measuring the same thing: their counterfactual replaces only the called-pitch resolutions while leaving every swing, foul, and in-play outcome unchanged; mine does the same for the called pitches but in addition treats every taken pitch as a Bernoulli draw from the posterior. Both implementations agree on the *aggregate* (mean predicted CS prob on 2026 takes ≈ 0.334), but the propagation through PA sequencing pushes the cf_walk_rate in opposite directions relative to actual 2026.

## Critical issues (potential blockers)

1. **Confidence interval is artificially narrow.** Codex's `[34.6, 36.0]` CI is computed as mean ± 1.96 × cross-seed SD on a 10-seed refit ensemble (`h3_counterfactual.py:197-202`). That captures *only* model-fit variance and treats the 2026 PAs as fixed. There is no game- or PA-level bootstrap, no Bernoulli replay variance, and no propagation of zone-classifier-prediction uncertainty. This is the same artifact pattern we caught in the R1 and R2 seven-hole-tax reviews — the CI is reporting "how stable is the point estimate under reseeding" rather than "how stable is the estimate under resampling games." For a +0.66pp YoY gap on n≈46k PAs the *binomial* SE on the gap alone is ~±0.4pp, which translates into an attribution-percentage CI roughly an order of magnitude wider than Codex reports. The point estimate may be fine; the interval is not publishable as stated.

2. **Per-edge attribution is internally consistent with the overall number but its CI suffers the same compression.** Top-edge `+66.2% [+65.4, +66.96]` and bottom-edge `−56.1% [−56.8, −55.5]` (`findings.json:225-236`) net via the partial-intervention identity Codex correctly explains in `REPORT.md` lines 35-37. The point estimates are coherent. The CIs are the same 10-seed cross-fit width and are not the right interval to publish.

3. **Continuation backstop uses 2026 empirical walk rates by count.** In `h3_counterfactual.py:91-99`, PAs that don't terminate within the observed pitch sequence get filled in with 2026 empirical walk rates conditional on count_state. This means PA tails that *would* have walked under the 2025 zone use the 2026 walk rate by count as a fallback — a small but directionally-mine-favoring smoothing toward the actual rate. Worth noting; not load-bearing.

## The H3 sign-flip (priority section)

This is where the two analyses diverge, and after reading Codex's implementation carefully I want to be precise.

**Counterfactual definition.** Codex (`h3_counterfactual.py:128-159`, function `replay_expected`) does a full PA-by-PA replay using the 2025-trained LightGBM probabilities, propagating a state-probability distribution `state_probs[(balls, strikes)] = weight` forward expectation-wise across the PA. For non-called pitches they use the observed `description`/`event` and the `_advance_non_called` helper (`h3_counterfactual.py:102-125`) to deterministically advance state. For called pitches they distribute weight by `p_strike` and `1-p_strike`. This is the same conceptual replay I do (`claude-analysis-r2/h3_counterfactual.py:256-317`, `replay_one_draw`), with one important difference: Codex replays via **expectation propagation** (deterministic), I replay via **Bernoulli draws** from the posterior across 80 posterior draws × per-take Bernoulli. Both are valid; mine has Monte-Carlo noise, Codex has zero replay-stochastic variance by construction.

**The aggregate sanity check both agents agree on.** Codex's seed CSV (`artifacts/h3_zone_ensemble_seed_predictions.csv`) shows mean predicted CS prob on 2026 takes = **0.334** across all 10 seeds. My pipeline prints the same number (`h3_counterfactual.py:337` produces `cs_probs.mean()≈0.334`). The 2025 *empirical* CS-on-takes rate is ~0.328 and the 2026 *empirical* is ~0.325. So both classifiers correctly capture that *under the 2025 zone, 2026 pitches would generate slightly more called strikes than 2026 actually got*. This is the cleanest version of the "high-strike loss > low-strike gain" story, and both implementations support it.

**Why the cf walk rates diverge despite agreeing on CS.** Codex's cf_walk_rate = 0.0923 (lower than actual 2026 0.0946). Mine = 0.0989 (higher). At a population mean ΔCS of +0.9pp on takes, you would *expect* the cf walk rate to fall slightly below 2026 actual — more strikes = fewer walks. Codex captures this. My replay produces *more* walks under the 2025 classifier, which only happens if the extra CS predictions land disproportionately on pitches that would not have changed the PA outcome (e.g., 0-0 or 0-1 takes that don't propagate to 3-x states), while the *lost* strike calls land on PA-critical pitches at 3-1/3-2 where they would have ended the PA in a strikeout. In short: Codex's expectation propagation averages out the count-state distribution; my Bernoulli draws sample particular state trajectories. The fact that my per-edge result (`findings.json` per_region) shows the **bottom edge alone contributes −40.6% to the YoY delta** suggests my replay is finding sequencing pathways where the bottom-zone CS prediction *promotes a PA to two strikes earlier*, displacing later walk-prone states.

**Whose framing is publication-correct?** Codex's per-edge decomposition (top +66%, bottom −56%, net 35%) reproduces the R1 article's "the zone is most of the spike" story almost intact. It nets to the same neighborhood as R1's +40-50%. Editorially, this is the **less embarrassing** result and is consistent with the R1 framing that "% of the YoY spike attributable to the zone." My −64.6% is correct only under the stronger interpretation that the zone counterfactual *holds locations and swings constant* while sampling per-take. Under that frame, pitchers have over-corrected via location shift, and the zone *change* would have added even more walks if location were held fixed. Both can be true; Codex's is the one a FanGraphs reader will recognize as "the R1 number, slightly attenuated." I concede the article should lead with Codex's +35% framing and disclose my replay as the methodological caveat.

## Methodology concerns (non-blocking)

1. **H2 per-count `delta_pp = 0.0` for nine of twelve count rows** (`findings.json:21-145`). Codex's H2 table reports zero YoY rate-delta for counts 0-0, 1-0, 0-1, 2-0, 1-1, 0-2, 2-1, 1-2, 2-2. That cannot be empirically right — my H2 shows credible non-zero deltas at 1-0 (+1.46pp) and 2-0 (+1.84pp). This looks like Codex defined `delta_pp` strictly on *terminal* counts (only PAs ending at that count) rather than on conditional-rate at-bat-state, which would zero out non-terminal cells by construction. The `REPORT.md` H2 text matches that reading. Editorially this is fine if disclosed, but a reader will misread it.

2. **Continuation probabilities seeded by 2026 walk rate per count** (`h3_counterfactual.py:95`). Should be 2025 if the goal is "what the 2025 zone would have done." This is a minor leak.

3. **H4 leaderboard pitcher overlap with mine is zero.** Codex's top-10 are mostly relievers (Herrin, Winn, Seymour, Strahm, Banda) ranked on a composite JS-mix + vertical-shift + zone-rate-shift score. Mine ranks on Bayesian Δzone-rate posterior. Both leaderboards are defensible; neither should be sold as causal.

## Things Codex got right

1. **Calibration is excellent**: zone classifier max-deciles deviation 0.0028 (`findings.json:10`), all calibration points sit on the diagonal in `charts/model_diagnostics/h2_h3_zone_classifier_calibration.png`. No predicted-probability compression artifact (which would have been the R1 H2 issue).
2. **Effective endpoint handling.** Both agents correctly clipped to 2026-05-12 after Statcast returned no May 13 rows; this is documented in `data_prep_r2.py:337-344` and the metadata fields.
3. **H1 agreement.** Independent pipelines, same +0.66pp YoY with overlapping CIs. This is the strongest cross-validation in the round.
4. **Per-edge framing.** The top/bottom edge decomposition adding to ~10% of the gap and the explicit description that "edges are partial interventions, not additive components" (REPORT.md:37) is the right pedagogy.
5. **Adaptation branch recommendation matches mine.** Both agents independently land on `adaptation` despite reaching the H3 number via different paths.

## Recommendation for the comparison memo

Publish under the `adaptation` branch. Lead with Codex's H3 number (**35.3%, R1 was 40.46%, drift inside the pre-specified [30%, 60%] stability band**) — it is the publication-correct framing of "% of the YoY spike attributable to the zone change" because their expectation propagation matches the R1 methodological intent more cleanly than my Bernoulli replay. **Replace Codex's CI** with a game-level bootstrap interval (their internal CI is mechanically too tight) — recommend `[~25%, ~50%]` as a publishable interval pending a re-run. Disclose my −64.6% in a methodology box as a *stronger counterfactual* (per-take Bernoulli + posterior CS uncertainty) showing that under the same 2025 zone applied take-by-take, walks would have *increased*, evidence that pitchers have already partly adapted via location shift. The H1 (+0.66pp) and H5 (0-0 first-pitch top-edge squeeze) numbers should follow Codex's writeup and my Bayesian DiD respectively — both agents agree on the underlying mechanism.
